If you are an Iranian student doing your PhD in Canada, and you are thinking about applying for internships, I want to share a few things I learned the hard way. This is not a rant, though I will admit some of it was frustrating. It is more of a honest heads-up, written with the hope that it helps someone else navigate this more smoothly than I did.

### The residency wall

Canada is generally a welcoming country, but it has a quirk that catches a lot of international students off guard: it is very difficult to obtain any form of permanent residency before you finish your PhD. That sounds fine on paper until you realize how much of the professional world is gated behind it.

Without permanent residency, you are considered a foreign national, and that classification has real consequences when it comes to internships.

### US export control laws reach into Canada

Here is the part that surprised me most. Even if you are applying to a company based in Canada, or anywhere outside the United States, US export control regulations can still apply. These laws restrict access to certain technologies and research areas for nationals of specific countries, and Iran is on that list.

In practice, this means companies will sometimes reject your application outright, even after you have cleared interviews. I experienced this personally with Amazon and Meta. You do well in the process, things feel promising, and then you get a short email explaining they cannot proceed due to export control compliance. It is a cold way to find out.

### It is not just US companies

You might think the solution is to apply exclusively to non-American companies, and that is a reasonable instinct. But if a company has any operations in the United States, even just an office or a partnership, they can still be subject to the same regulations and may need to apply for a US export license on your behalf before hiring you for certain roles.

This is not a hypothetical. It happened to me with Torc Robotics and with AMI, Yann LeCun's startup. Both were situations where things were going well, and then the export license question became a blocker. Some companies will pursue the license, many will not, often depending on their legal budget or internal policy.

### So where does that leave you?

Realistically, your options narrow to a few paths. Large tech companies like Google and NVIDIA tend to have the legal infrastructure and resources to navigate export licensing, and some actively do it for international candidates. If you can land something there, that is genuinely a strong outcome.

Beyond that, you are mostly looking at Canadian companies with no US exposure, which sounds like a clean solution until you look at the robotics landscape here.

### The local Canadian robotics scene

I want to be fair here because there are good people building real things in Canada. But if you are doing cutting-edge research in robot learning, diffusion policies, world models, or anything at the frontier of the field, most Canadian robotics companies are not there yet. The research directions often lag by several years, and the work you would be doing as an intern is unlikely to connect meaningfully with your thesis. If you are the kind of person who cares deeply about staying at the edge of what is possible, which I suspect you are if you are reading a research blog, then these roles can feel like a detour.

### What I wish I had known earlier

Start the conversation with a company about export licensing early, before you invest time in interviews. A simple question in the first email or screening call can save weeks of uncertainty.

Focus your energy on companies known to sponsor export licenses, or on fully Canadian operations with no US presence. And if you are applying broadly, be mentally prepared for the rejection that comes from compliance, not from your work. It has nothing to do with how good you are.

This is a structural problem, not a personal one. I hope it gets easier.
